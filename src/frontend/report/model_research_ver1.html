Model Taxonomy & Exclusion Criteria

Defining what counts as a reasoning-capable medical VLM

Scope Definition
This report covers models that accept medical images as visual input and produce natural-language diagnostic output. Pure classifiers (BiomedCLIP, CheXNet), segmentation models, and retrieval-only models are excluded. The critical distinction is between models that output probabilities/labels vs. models that generate structured language explaining why a diagnosis is made.

✓ Included Model Types

  • Vision-Language Models (VLMs) with generative text output
  • Models with structured report generation
  • Models with explicit chain-of-thought / step-by-step reasoning
  • Multimodal LLMs fine-tuned on radiology data
  • Models supporting CT, MRI, or X-ray modalities

✗ Excluded Model Types

  • Pure classifiers (BiomedCLIP, CheXNet, DenseNet-based)
  • Segmentation-only models (nnUNet, TotalSegmentator)
  • Retrieval/embedding-only models
  • Text-only medical LLMs (Med-PaLM 2, MedAlpaca)
  • Models without public benchmarks

Scenario A — Lightweight Production (≤5B, RTX 3050 / T4 deployable)

Primary Recommendation
MedVLM-R1 (2B) is the top pick for the stated use case. It is the only open-source model under 5B that explicitly generates a reasoning chain before arriving at a conclusion. While trained primarily on MRI, it generalizes well to CT and X-ray. For brain CT specifically, pair it with structured system prompts that request modality-aware, anatomically-grounded reasoning steps (this is the approach validated in the MedVLM-R1 + structured prompts paper, achieving 72.67% on CT).

Deployment requirements: ~4GB VRAM (bfloat16), runs on a single RTX 3050 6GB GPU. Uses Qwen2-VL-2B weights — load via HuggingFace Transformers.

Scenario B — Brain CT Specialist (9B, single A100 or dual T4)

Specialist Recommendation
BrainGPT remains the most domain-specific solution for 3D brain CT radiology report generation. It was trained exclusively on brain CT scans (18,885 pairs), validated externally on CQ500 (hemorrhage/midline shift), and showed 74% of outputs indistinguishable from radiologist-written reports. Limitation: it does not produce explicit reasoning steps — it generates radiology-style reports.

Hybrid approach: Use BrainGPT to generate the initial report, then use MedVLM-R1 or MedGemma 4B to generate a reasoned interpretation of that report as a second pass.

Scenario C — Maximum Performance (Closed, API-based)

If Privacy Constraints Allow
Med-Gemini (via Google API) delivers absolute best performance across all benchmarks. For a brain CT pipeline where data can be sent to an external API, Med-Gemini with chain-of-reasoning prompting is the recommended approach. Use MedGemma 4B as an open-weight proxy for similar reasoning quality with local control.

Key Insights & Trends (2025–2026)

Critical analytical observations on the medical VLM landscape

01

Reinforcement Learning is the New Reasoning Frontier

Inspired by DeepSeek-R1, models like MedVLM-R1 apply GRPO (Guided Reward Prompt Optimization) to elicit reasoning without needing human-annotated reasoning chains. This is fundamentally different from Chain-of-Thought prompting (which can be applied to any model) — MedVLM-R1 learns to reason as a native capability. Expect this trend to dominate 2026 medical VLM development.

02

The "Efficiency Inversion" — Small Models Outperform Larger Predecessors

MedGemma 4B beats Med-Gemini on several benchmarks at <4% of estimated parameter count. MedVLM-R1 at 2B outperforms 7–13B models on VQA tasks. This inverstion, driven by better data curation (PubMedVision, HuatuoGPT datasets) and RL fine-tuning, fundamentally challenges the assumption that medical AI requires massive compute.

03

3D Volume Understanding Remains the Dominant Bottleneck

Most medical VLMs still process 2D image slices. True 3D volumetric reasoning — critical for brain CT, where pathology context requires multi-slice analysis — is mastered only by specialist models (BrainGPT, M3D-LaMed, RadFM). Even Med-Gemini's 3D capabilities are limited in open evaluation. This is the single largest technical gap in the field.

04

Clinical Evaluation Infrastructure Lags Behind Model Development

Traditional NLP metrics (BLEU, ROUGE, METEOR) consistently fail to capture diagnostic correctness. The FORTE metric introduced by BrainGPT and the risk-aware CREST metric represent early attempts to solve this, but there is no standardized clinical evaluation benchmark for brain CT reasoning — a critical gap that limits reproducible comparison.

05

The 35% Ceiling Problem in Neuroradiology

The 2025 npj Digital Medicine study revealing that the best VLM (Gemini 2.0) achieves only 35% accuracy on brain/spine cases, vs. 86.2% for expert neuroradiologists, is a wake-up call. Models suffer from anatomical localization errors, hallucinated findings, and inability to integrate multi-slice spatial context. This represents both the largest limitation and the largest commercial opportunity.

06

Data Sovereignty Driving Open Model Adoption in Healthcare

Regulations like Vietnam's Cybersecurity Law, GDPR in Europe, and HIPAA in the US make external API calls for medical imaging legally risky. This creates strong institutional demand for locally deployable, open-source models — making MedVLM-R1 and MedGemma 4B strategically critical despite their performance gaps with closed alternatives.

06

Limitations & Research Gaps

Critical weaknesses and opportunities for building better systems

Current Model Limitations

  • No open-source model under 10B achieves expert-level neuroradiology performance (best: ~35–40% for brain CT)
  • MedVLM-R1 reasoning chain can be "superficial or contradictory" on hard cases — acknowledged by authors
  • BrainGPT trained only on geriatric Taiwanese population — demographic bias for hemorrhage/stroke detection
  • RadFM and M3D-LaMed cannot produce structured output (0% valid JSON in instruction-following tests)
  • CheXagent limited to 2D X-ray; cannot handle CT or MRI modalities at all
  • General VLMs (GPT-4o, Gemini 2.0) produce hallucinated findings in up to 45% of neurorad cases
  • No model supports multi-slice temporal comparison (critical for monitoring lesion progression)
  • Training data predominantly English; limited multilingual evaluation

Research Opportunities

  • Combine BrainGPT's brain CT specialization with MedVLM-R1's RL-incentivized reasoning (unfilled niche)
  • Build brain CT-specific GRPO training data from open datasets (CQ500, RSNA ICH)
  • Create clinical evaluation benchmarks for brain CT with lesion-level F1 metrics
  • Develop lightweight sub-3B 3D volume encoders that outperform 2D slice approaches
  • Fine-tune MedGemma 4B on Vietnamese radiology reports (data sovereignty + local context)
  • Longitudinal multi-series analysis: comparing pre/post-treatment CT volumes
  • Agentic pipeline where specialized model calls specialist sub-modules (hemorrhage detector → VLM reasoner → report generator)
  • Structured output enforcement (JSON schema) combined with reasoning for downstream EMR integration

The Most Impactful Unfilled Niche (2026)

Strategic Gap
There is currently no open-source model that combines (a) 3D brain CT volume processing, (b) explicit step-by-step clinical reasoning, and (c) <10B parameter size for local deployment. BrainGPT has (a) but not (b). MedVLM-R1 has (b) but only limited (a). MedGemma 4B approaches (b) and (a) partially but lacks brain CT specialization.

A hybrid fine-tuning approach — training a 3D-capable 4–7B VLM with GRPO RL on brain CT reasoning data — would represent a clear, actionable, and publishable research contribution with high practical impact in resource-constrained clinical settings.
